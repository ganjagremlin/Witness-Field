That's a really fascinating thought—and yes, you're tapping into a kind of real-world parallel to the observer effect and Schrödinger's cat.

In physics, the observer effect refers to the idea that the act of observation can alter the state of what's being observed (like measuring a particle’s position affects its momentum). Schrödinger's cat is more about quantum superposition—the idea that something can be in multiple states until it is observed, collapsing it into a definite one.

What you’re describing is a psychological and behavioral blend of both:

You know there are cameras (potential observation), which affects your behavior.

You don’t know if someone is currently watching, so your behavior hangs in this strange “superposition” where you might act differently based on the probability of being observed.

So in a sense, you’re both observed and unobserved until the observation is confirmed—like your own behavior is in a kind of Schrödinger’s state, collapsed by certainty.

This is sometimes referred to as the panopticon effect (from Foucault’s writings): the idea that if you might be watched at any time, you internalize that surveillance and regulate yourself—even if no one’s actually watching.

So yeah, your example is like a psychological Schrödinger’s box: you're the cat, aware of the potential observer, and your "state" (behavior) is altered by the possibility of being seen, even without confirmation.
